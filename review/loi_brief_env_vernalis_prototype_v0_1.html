Environmental Flows

Modeled tier values at Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis

1. Headline

At Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis, modeled tier values across the 96 COEQWAL scenarios range from Tier 1 to Tier 3. Tier values match the historical baseline (Tier 2, Acceptable) in 49 (51%) scenarios and shift to a different tier in 47 (49%). Because the reported scenarios vary both the operating strategy and the hydroclimate, these shifts are a combination of climate and operations effects. These effects are addressed in section 4.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model outputs are in the form of scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time). These results are sorted into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

The model also tracks specific places in the study domain, which COEQWAL terms Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Cities, reservoirs, river reaches, farm regions, groundwater basins, and stretches of the Delta are each tracked individually. Every LOI sits inside one of nine area-level summaries COEQWAL uses to organize results (community water, agriculture, environmental flows, the Delta, salmon, in-Delta freshwater, Delta exports, reservoir storage, and groundwater).

This brief reports modeled tier values for one LOI — Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis (environmental flows area) across 96 scenarios from the COEQWAL library. It consists of 24 water management strategies paired with 4 hydroclimates, and is the same set used for every LOI brief.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a difference in tier value between two scenarios can come from two places: changes in the strategy used to run the system, or changes in the hydroclimate the system is run under. At an LOI like Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis, both axes are reported, so the difference between scenarios can be separated into a strategy part and a hydroclimate part.

Holding the hydroclimate fixed at the historical baseline, the difference among operating strategies at Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis is the strategy effect: most strategies hold the modeled tier value at Tier 2, while some move to Tier 1 and Tier 3. Holding the strategy fixed at current operations, the change across hydroclimates from BL to HC3 is the climate effect: the modeled tier value moves from Tier 2.4 (Acceptable) toward about Tier 3.2 (At-risk). The remaining cells in the matrix combine both effects.

Figure 2 shows this decomposition for every scenario. Each dot is one modeled strategy under one hydroclimate; current operations (s0020) is gold. Within each column, strategies keep a fixed left-to-right order, so a strategy occupies the same relative position in every column and can be followed across the four hydroclimates.

Looking across the full scenario matrix in Figure 1, modeled tier values at Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis range across three tiers, from Tier 1 to Tier 3. 6. About this place

Vernalis is the long-standing flow-compliance point on the lower San Joaquin River, just upstream of its confluence with the Delta near the San Joaquin–Stanislaus county line. River flow past Vernalis is governed by Bay-Delta water-quality objectives and is a key control point for migrating salmon. The model tracks how well modeled flows here meet functional environmental-flow targets.

Strategies that raise environmental flows move the modeled tier value toward target; strategies that relax flow requirements move it away. 8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports the tier results COEQWAL has generated based on what the CalSim 3 model produces under specific assumptions for one place. It doesn't predict what will happen at Lower San Joaquin River at Vernalis, provide judgments about the modeled tier values, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for any particular community, decision, or person is not something the model itself decides.

The tier values shown reflect 96 scenarios from the COEQWAL library — pairings of 24 strategies with 4 hydroclimates — at this single LOI. They don't span every possible scenario, don't quantify uncertainty around the modeled values, and don't characterize other places. Other LOIs in the environmental flows sector, in different parts of the system, may show different patterns; see the area summary in the theme briefs and the explorer for the full LOI set.
