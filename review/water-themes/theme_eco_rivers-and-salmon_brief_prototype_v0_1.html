COEQWAL Theme Brief · Public
Rivers and Salmon · Public Brief

Across the strategies COEQWAL modeled, no single flow strategy helps every part of the river at once: protecting cold water for winter-run salmon and sending more water down the river draw on the same supply — and a hotter climate sharpens the conflict.

1. Headline

COEQWAL's water model paired several ways of managing California's rivers with a range of hydroclimates, from a historical baseline to hotter, drier futures. One pattern recurs: cold water stored behind a reservoir like Shasta keeps winter-run Chinook eggs alive in summer, while river flows released earlier in the year carry young salmon out to sea — and the same limited water can't do both. A strategy that protects the cold-water pool can leave less for outmigration flows; a strategy that boosts flows can draw the cold-water pool down and raise egg mortality. Under hotter, drier hydroclimates there is less cold water to go around, and these trade-offs grow.

Headline distilled from the public theme narrative and the workshop doc's three lenses (Trade-offs, Distributional equity, Resilience). Directional only; magnitudes held as stubs pending confirmed tier values.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model runs as scenarios: each scenario pairs a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) with a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that set how much water is available over time).

The model sorts results into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

This brief looks at the rivers and salmon theme: how [N] strategies affect seasonal river flows, reservoir storage, winter-run Chinook survival across its life stages, outflows to the Bay-Delta estuary, and water deliveries to farms and communities. The strategies tested include flows timed to mimic natural river patterns (environmental and functional flows), protections for cold-water storage and carryover at Shasta, reduced irrigated acreage, removing minimum flow requirements, and limits on groundwater pumping. A key idea explored here is that river flows can be managed to better resemble natural patterns while still delivering water to people.

Strategy families from the source docs; raw scenario codes (s0030–s0033, s0044, s0046) dropped from public copy and parked in §5/§7 devnotes.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a change in salmon outcomes could come from two places: the strategy used to run the system, or the hydroclimate it runs under. For this theme, both matter, and they can be read separately.

The operations — whether the system protects the cold-water pool, releases functional flows, or reduces irrigated acreage — set the basic trade-off between salmon life stages. The climate then acts on top. A hotter, drier hydroclimate holds less cold water behind Shasta, so the same strategy yields worse winter-run outcomes, and the gap between the best and worst strategies narrows as the climate dries. Functional-flow strategies also lose ground as the number of dry years rises. Read against the BASELINES brief — which holds today's strategy constant and so isolates the climate-only part — this theme shows what changes when the management strategy itself changes.

Workshop "Resilience" lens: average environmental-flow tiers worsen from historical to future hydroclimates (worst under TaiESM1); reduced-acreage + broad functional-flow strategies stay most stable; salmon-protection-only and groundwater-restriction scenarios degrade most. For winter-run, the cold-water-refuge scenario (s0033) is best under the historical hydroclimate but drops fastest under cc50/cc95 as cold water gets scarcer.

5. The pattern across scenarios

Several patterns show up consistently. First, cold water and river flow pull against each other: prioritizing the cold-water pool behind Shasta improves egg survival but can leave less flow for juvenile outmigration, while increasing flows helps juveniles but draws the cold-water pool down and raises egg mortality. Because winter-run depend on every life stage, a strategy that helps one stage but harms another can still leave the population worse off.

Second, the outcomes differ in how much they move. River-flow conditions (environmental flows) are the most responsive — they span a wide range of tiers across strategies, because that outcome is tied directly to the flow targets the model scales on. Winter-run and estuary outcomes move far less and tend to stay in the lower tiers, because they also depend on things flows alone don't control: the cold-water pool, habitat, and net Delta outflow. Third, functional flows help smolt outmigration but do not, on their own, lift the overall winter-run outcome.

A caution comes with these readings. A tier is an average across many individual places, each counted equally regardless of size. A strategy can show a small average change while a few places move a great deal — the summary reflects how evenly results are spread, not the total effect. The data explorer is the place to see the size of the change, location by location.

Workshop "Trade-offs" + "Distributional equity" lenses. eflows are the most sensitive outcome (multiple LOIs, hydrologic scaling); winter-run and estuary perform poorly/consistently. Functional flows help smolt outmigration but don't register in the winter-run outcome (multi-lifestage stressors, lost rearing grounds). Anything that depletes Shasta cold-water storage harms winter-run.

6. About specific places

The theme summarizes many specific places across California — what the model calls Locations of Interest, or LOIs: river reaches, tributaries, and the reservoirs that feed them. Where a flow strategy helps or hurts depends a great deal on which places it touches.

The model shows tributaries responding more sharply than the lower mainstem. In the Sacramento basin the Yuba River is the most sensitive to changes in strategy; in the San Joaquin basin the Tuolumne River is. Lower-mainstem locations such as Vernalis and Verona stay more stable. Some of that tributary swing reflects how the model balances flows internally as much as real operational flexibility, so it is worth reading tributary results with care. Winter-run survival, by contrast, hinges on cold water in the Sacramento system below Shasta — which is why it tracks cold-water storage more than any single flow target.

Because a tier averages across all the places in an area, an area can look stable at the summary level while individual reaches are under real stress. To see how a particular river or reservoir is modeled, the data explorer lets you look one Location of Interest at a time. This brief stays at the summary level.

Workshop "Distributional equity" lens: cluster LOIs into lower mainstem / Sac tributary / SJ tributary; Yuba most sensitive in the Sacramento, Tuolumne in the San Joaquin; mainstem more stable. Yuba's swing attributed partly to internal CalSim weighting.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports what the model produces under specific assumptions. It doesn't predict what will happen, judge whether the results are acceptable, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for a particular river, community, or decision is not something the model itself decides.

Improving flows alone does not fully restore a river ecosystem. Habitat conditions, landscape changes, and invasive species also shape outcomes, and COEQWAL does not evaluate them directly — so a strategy that scores poorly on flows here may do better when paired with habitat restoration or other actions. Tiers also describe how results are spread across places, not the total effect; each Location of Interest counts equally regardless of size. For the size of a change, and for any single place, see §6 and the data explorer.

Carries the narrative's two key caveats: flows alone don't restore ecosystems (habitat/invasives not modeled), and the LOI-averaging limit shared across briefs.
