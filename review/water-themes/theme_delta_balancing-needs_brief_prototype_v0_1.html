Balancing needs in the Delta · Public Brief

Across the strategies COEQWAL modeled, the Delta can't maximize everything at once: water held for storage or sent to farms and cities competes with the outflow that holds salinity back and supports the estuary — and rising seas and drought tighten the squeeze.

1. Headline

COEQWAL's water model paired several ways of managing California's water with a range of hydroclimates, from a historical baseline to hotter, drier futures. The Sacramento–San Joaquin Delta sits at the center of the system: the same water can be held in reservoirs, exported to farms and cities, or sent out through the Delta to push back salt and support the estuary — but not all at once. Increasing outflow for the estuary changes storage and exports; protecting storage changes how much flows downstream; managing the pumps shifts where salt sits in the channels. Rising seas and longer droughts make every one of these trade-offs harder.

Headline distilled from the public theme narrative's three lenses (Trade-offs, Equity, Resilience). Directional only — no scenario-level findings exist for delta yet; magnitudes held as stubs.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model runs as scenarios: each scenario pairs a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) with a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that set how much water is available over time).

The model sorts results into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

This brief looks at the balancing needs in the Delta theme: how [N] strategies affect freshwater available to in-Delta farms and communities, the Delta outflows that support estuary health, reservoir storage north and south of the Delta, and the freshwater exported from the Delta to farms and cities elsewhere in the state. The Delta is at once infrastructure — levees, pumps, channels — and a living estuary, and these strategies move water through both.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a change in Delta conditions could come from two places: the strategy used to run the system, or the hydroclimate it runs under. For this theme, both matter, and they can be read separately.

The operations — how much water is released as Delta outflow, how much is exported, how much storage is held north and south of the Delta, and how the pumps are run — set the basic balance between the estuary, in-Delta users, and exports. The climate then acts on top, and in the Delta it brings an extra lever: sea-level rise. Rising seas push saltwater farther inland, so under hotter, drier hydroclimates it takes more outflow just to hold salinity where it is today — leaving less for storage and exports. Some water-quality standards must legally be met, so a few outcomes can look stable even as the rest of the system shifts around them. Read against the BASELINES brief — which holds today's strategy constant — this theme shows what changes when the management strategy itself changes.

5. The pattern across scenarios

A few patterns recur. First, water is limited, so supporting one goal affects another: increasing outflow to improve estuary conditions changes storage and deliveries; protecting storage changes how much moves downstream; and managing the pumps shifts where salt sits in the Delta. The Delta is where these trade-offs become visible.

Second, the Delta responds unevenly to small moves. A modest change in flow or pumping can improve habitat for native species in one part of the Delta while making conditions more stressful in another — there is rarely a single change that helps everywhere. Third, outflow is not a master switch: changing Delta outflow alone does not restore the estuary's health, because levees, habitat, land use, and invasive species — which the model does not directly evaluate — also shape outcomes.

A caution comes with these readings. A tier is an average across many individual places, each counted equally regardless of size. A strategy can show a small average change while a few places move a great deal — the summary reflects how evenly results are spread, not the total effect. The data explorer is the place to see the detail, location by location.

6. About specific places

The theme summarizes many specific places across the Delta and the reservoirs that serve it — what the model calls Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Different parts of the Delta experience the same strategy differently, and a benefit in one area can create pressure in another.

Storage held north of the Delta and storage held south of it respond differently to the same strategy, because they serve different demands and sit on different sides of the pumps. In-Delta farms and communities depend on freshwater staying fresh where they are, which makes places closer to the advancing saltwater front more exposed as seas rise. Where you sit in the Delta — and what water rights you hold — shapes whether a given strategy reads as a benefit or a burden.

Because a tier averages across all the places in an area, an area can look stable at the summary level while individual islands or reaches are under real stress. To see how a particular part of the Delta is modeled, the data explorer lets you look one Location of Interest at a time. This brief stays at the summary level.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports what the model produces under specific assumptions. It doesn't predict what will happen, judge whether the results are acceptable, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for a particular community, farm, or decision is not something the model itself decides.

Changing Delta outflow alone does not restore the estuary's health. Landscape conditions, habitat, levees, and invasive species also shape outcomes, and COEQWAL does not evaluate them directly. Some water-quality standards must legally be met, so certain outcomes may look stable even when other parts of the system change. Tiers describe how results are spread across places, not the total effect; each Location of Interest counts equally regardless of size. For the detail, and for any single place, see §6 and the data explorer.
