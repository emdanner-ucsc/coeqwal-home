COEQWAL Theme Brief · Public
Farms and groundwater · Public Brief

Across the strategies COEQWAL modeled, rebuilding groundwater or sending more water to rivers and the Delta tends to lower agricultural revenue — and a drier climate sharpens that trade-off.

1. Headline

COEQWAL's water model paired several ways of managing California's farms and groundwater with a range of hydroclimates, from a historical baseline to hotter, drier futures. One pattern recurs: the strategies that rebuild groundwater storage, or set aside more water for rivers and the Delta, tend to lower agricultural revenue at the same time. Where those costs land is uneven — the San Joaquin Valley and the Sacramento Valley are affected differently depending on the strategy. And under a drier climate, the trade-off between farm revenue and water for the environment grows larger.

Headline distilled from the source doc's three analytical lenses (Trade-offs, Distributional equity, Resilience) and its own theme-narrative action-item summary. Directional claims only; magnitudes held as stubs pending confirmed tier values (see §8 on the averaging caveat).

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model runs as scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time).

The model sorts results into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

This brief looks at the farms and groundwater theme: how [N] strategies affect two things in particular — the revenue California agriculture earns and the amount of water held in storage in the state's groundwater basins. The strategies tested include limiting how much groundwater is pumped, reducing irrigated acreage, setting aside water for rivers and ecosystems (environmental and functional flows), and sending more water out through the Delta. Limits on groundwater pumping are modeled in part because the Sustainable Groundwater Management Act (SGMA) requires basins to stop over-drafting groundwater over time.

Scope paragraph lists the strategy families from the source doc (pumping limits, acreage reductions, environmental/functional flows, increased Delta outflows). Raw scenario codes (s0020, s0025–s0033, s0039) dropped from public copy. SGMA mention kept brief and factual — explains why pumping limits are a strategy worth modeling, without advocacy.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a change in farm revenue could come from two places: the strategy used to run the system, or the hydroclimate it runs under. For this theme, both matter, and they can be read separately.

Most of the trade-off comes from operations — from the choice to limit groundwater pumping, reduce irrigated acreage, or route more water to the environment. Those are management decisions, and they move farm revenue regardless of the weather. The climate then acts on top of that. The model shows that a hotter, drier hydroclimate makes the environmental-flow and Delta-outflow strategies cost agriculture more than they do under the historical baseline, while the effect of groundwater pumping limits on revenue is more stable across hydroclimates. Read against the BASELINES brief — which holds today's strategy constant and so isolates the climate-only part — this theme shows what changes when the management strategy itself changes.

Source "Resilience" lens: climate change increases the impact of environmental flows and Delta outflows on agricultural revenue; pumping-limit scenarios show little additional worsening under the high-climate-risk run. The ops-vs-climate split is real here (unlike BASELINES, where the strategy is held constant).

5. The pattern across scenarios

Three trade-offs show up consistently. First, rebuilding groundwater and sustaining farm revenue pull against each other: strategies that limit pumping and reduce irrigated acreage improve groundwater storage but lower agricultural revenue. The clearest version is when pumping limits and acreage reductions are applied together; with pumping limits alone, groundwater storage south of the Delta doesn't clearly improve.

Second, water for the environment and farm revenue pull against each other: strategies that set aside more water for rivers and ecosystems improve environmental conditions but lower agricultural revenue in both the northern and southern parts of the system. Third, flows timed to mimic natural river patterns (functional flows) improve river conditions while drawing down reservoir storage.

A caution comes with these readings. A tier is an average across many individual places, and every place counts equally regardless of its size. So a strategy can show a small average change while a few large places move a great deal — the summary reflects how evenly results are spread, not the total volume of water or revenue involved. The data explorer is the place to see the size of the change, location by location.

Source "Trade-offs" lens, plus the recurring methodology caveat (tiers weight all Locations of Interest equally, so averages reflect spatial equity rather than total volume). 6. About specific places

The theme summarizes many specific places across California — what the model calls Locations of Interest, or LOIs: farm regions, groundwater basins, and the water-budget areas they sit in. Where a strategy's costs and benefits land depends a great deal on which places it touches.

The model shows the San Joaquin Valley and the Sacramento Valley responding differently. Flows timed to mimic natural river patterns reduce farm revenue more in the San Joaquin Valley than in the Sacramento Valley — in part because water users there hold more junior water rights, so they give up water first. Sending more water out through the Delta, by contrast, reduces farm revenue more in the Sacramento Valley, with less change in the San Joaquin. Groundwater responds to where demand falls: south of the Delta, where farms rely more on groundwater, storage improves most when pumping is restricted; north of the Delta, where reliance is lower, storage changes less.

Because a tier averages across all the places in an area, an area can look stable at the summary level while individual places within it are under real stress. To see how a particular basin or farm region is modeled, the data explorer lets you look one Location of Interest at a time. This brief stays at the summary level.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports what the model produces under specific assumptions. It doesn't predict what will happen, judge whether the results are acceptable, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for a particular farm, community, or decision is not something the model itself decides.

Tiers also describe how results are spread across places, not the total volume of water or revenue at stake. Each Location of Interest counts equally in the average regardless of its size, so a large change concentrated in a few big places can be masked by a summary that looks modest — and a small change spread widely can look larger than it is. For the size of a change, and for any single place, see §6 and the data explorer.

Disclaimer carries the source's most-repeated methodological worry: averaging continuous tiers across equally weighted LOIs obscures total-volume effects. Kept here as a limit on reading the brief rather than as a methods discussion.
