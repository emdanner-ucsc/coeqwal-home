Community Water Systems

Modeled tier values at City of Stockton

1. Headline

At City of Stockton, modeled tier values across the 96 COEQWAL scenarios range from Tier 1 to Tier 3. Tier values match the historical baseline (Tier 2, Acceptable) in 50 (52%) scenarios and shift to a different tier in 46 (48%). Because the reported scenarios vary both the operating strategy and the hydroclimate, these shifts are a combination of climate and operations effects. These effects are addressed in section 4.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model outputs are in the form of scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time). These results are sorted into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

The model also tracks specific places in the study domain, which COEQWAL terms Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Cities, reservoirs, river reaches, farm regions, groundwater basins, and stretches of the Delta are each tracked individually. Every LOI sits inside one of nine area-level summaries COEQWAL uses to organize results (community water, agriculture, environmental flows, the Delta, salmon, in-Delta freshwater, Delta exports, reservoir storage, and groundwater).

This brief reports modeled tier values for one LOI — City of Stockton (community water systems area) across 96 scenarios from the COEQWAL library. It consists of 24 water management strategies paired with 4 hydroclimates, and is the same set used for every LOI brief.

Figure 1 shows the modeled tier value at City of Stockton across 24 modeled strategies and 4 modeled hydroclimates. Each gray line traces one strategy through the four hydroclimates. Line slope shows how that strategy's modeled tier value shifts under climate variation; the vertical spread among lines at a single hydroclimate shows the range of tier values from operational variation alone. 4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a difference in tier value between two scenarios can come from two places: changes in the strategy used to run the system, or changes in the hydroclimate the system is run under. At an LOI like City of Stockton, both axes are reported, so the difference between scenarios can be separated into a strategy part and a hydroclimate part.

Holding the hydroclimate fixed at the historical baseline, the difference among operating strategies at City of Stockton is the strategy effect: most strategies hold the modeled tier value at Tier 2, while some move to Tier 1. Holding the strategy fixed at current operations, the change across hydroclimates from BL to HC3 is the climate effect: the modeled tier value moves from Tier 2.0 toward about Tier 2.9, staying within Tier 2 (Acceptable). The remaining cells in the matrix combine both effects.

Figure 2 shows this decomposition for every scenario. Each dot is one modeled strategy under one hydroclimate; current operations (s0020) is gold. Within each column, strategies keep a fixed left-to-right order, so a strategy occupies the same relative position in every column and can be followed across the four hydroclimates.

Read against the BASELINES theme brief, the climate column at City of Stockton under current operations is the climate-only reference for this place. Read against the area summary for community water systems, the rows under alternative strategies show how operating choices shift tier values here.

5. Pattern across scenarios

Looking across the full scenario matrix in Figure 1, modeled tier values at City of Stockton range across three tiers, from Tier 1 to Tier 3. The variation across the four hydroclimates under any single strategy is broadly comparable to the variation across the 24 strategies under any single hydroclimate. The widest spread appears at Warmer-drier II (HC2) and Warmer-drier III (HC3), where modeled tier values under different strategies span from Tier 1 to Tier 3.

6. About this place

Stockton sits at the eastern edge of the Sacramento–San Joaquin Delta in San Joaquin County. Its community water systems are supplied by a mix of Delta surface water, Central Valley Project and New Melones contract deliveries, and local groundwater. The model tracks how reliably modeled deliveries meet the city's municipal and industrial (M&I) demand.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports the tier results COEQWAL has generated based on what the CalSim 3 model produces under specific assumptions for one place. It doesn't predict what will happen at City of Stockton, provide judgments about the modeled tier values, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for any particular community, decision, or person is not something the model itself decides.

The tier values shown reflect 96 scenarios from the COEQWAL library — pairings of 24 strategies with 4 hydroclimates — at this single LOI. They don't span every possible scenario, don't quantify uncertainty around the modeled values, and don't characterize other places. Other LOIs in the community water systems sector, in different parts of the system, may show different patterns; see the area summary in the theme briefs and the explorer for the full LOI set.
