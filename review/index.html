Before you read and edit

  • These are stand-alone documents. Each brief should make sense on its own, to a reader who has not seen the others, the website, or any of our meetings. When editing, please don't add wording that assumes that background — no "as discussed," no references to a sibling brief. If something needs context, the brief has to supply it.
  • These are format prototypes, not final copy. The most useful feedback right now is on structure, flow, and whether the framing works for a general reader — not polishing every sentence. The layout may still change.
  • Most numbers are stand-ins. Please don't fact-check the figures or react to specific values in the prototype briefs; their real model results are not wired in yet, and the briefs flagged with a caveat also use stand-in locations. The exceptions are the "real data" location briefs (Oroville, Yuba, Trinity) and the Shasta example linked from the findings page: those are built from real model results from the essentially complete dataset (July 2026). A final verification pass is still to come, so treat specific values as near-final rather than citable.
  • Written for the public. Plain language is the goal. Flag anything that reads as jargon or assumes the reader already knows the modeling.
  • The full set of sections is deliberate. Every brief keeps its complete structure; a section that does not apply to a given place is kept on purpose with a short note, rather than dropped. No need to flag those as missing.

What we need your input on

  1. What should the headline figure look like? Two candidate forms for the lead figure on a brief. One is a trade-off plot: two measures on two axes (for example, water for farms left-to-right and water for the environment bottom-to-top), so each scenario is a point and you can see what is traded against what. The other is a radial (wheel-shaped) plot, where each spoke is one of the nine sectors and the distance out the spoke shows how that sector scores. This choice affects all four themes, and the figures stay as placeholders until it is settled.
  2. Does the one location-brief format hold across different kinds of place? The Folsom brief is a reservoir. The three new location briefs apply the same nine-part format to a city (community water), a groundwater basin, and a river reach. Tell us where the shared structure works, and where a particular kind of place needs its own wording or a different figure.

How to give feedback. For general reactions, reply to the email this link came with, or leave a comment in the brief's Google Doc. To suggest specific wording, open the brief's Google Doc — linked under the three core briefs below — and edit it directly; turn on Suggesting mode so changes show as tracked suggestions. Several people can edit the same doc at once, so there is no file to send back.

Proposed edits to the baseline theme

Three small wording changes to the Understanding Today's Water System theme that keep it consistent with the Baselines reference brief above. The theme is the explainer that sits above the brief, so it carries the shared framing. These are suggested for the theme's source document.

Add a baseline-terminology paragraph §3, Box 3

Add to the end of Box 3:

Current operations carries two names, depending on the climate it runs in. Paired with the historical hydroclimate, it is the baseline — today's conditions as actually experienced. Paired with a future hydroclimate, it becomes a climate-projected baseline — where current operations would land if the climate changed but management did not. Both are the same operations; only the climate differs.

Why: "Baseline" is used two ways across the project — today's actual conditions, and the projected runs under future climates. Naming both here lets every page below use the short forms without confusion.

Add a "two senses of trade-off" paragraph §6, after Trade-offs

Add immediately after the existing Trade-offs paragraph:

Two senses of "trade-off" run through COEQWAL, and it helps to keep them apart. One is the standing trade-off — competing uses that exist at any moment, visible even when nothing about management changes. The other is the strategy trade-off — the cost of one management choice measured against another, which only becomes readable when you compare strategies. Current operations on its own shows the standing trade-offs and how climate sharpens them; reading a strategy trade-off requires the briefs where the management strategy changes.

Why: The Baselines brief states it shows no strategy trade-offs, because the strategy is held fixed there. Without this distinction, that looks like it contradicts the theme. Naming the two senses once makes both pages agree.

Point the reader to the Baselines brief §7, final paragraph

Replace the current closing sentence:

Understanding today's water system provides a foundation for interpreting every scenario explored throughout COEQWAL.

With

The modeled results for current operations — each of nine areas of the water system, across the historical baseline and the drier hydroclimates — are laid out in the Baselines brief; start there to see the pattern this theme describes. Understanding today's water system provides a foundation for interpreting every scenario explored throughout COEQWAL.

Why: It points the reader from the explainer to the page with the actual numbers, so the two are explicitly linked.

For reference — matching changes already in the brief

Two changes to the Baselines brief itself, already applied: a short opening paragraph naming this theme as its explainer and linking to it; and the line "there are no tradeoffs to read here" rewritten to "no strategy trade-offs… standing trade-offs among competing uses are still present," matching the §6 change above. One caveat: the brief's fourth column (Warmer & Drier III) is still a stand-in, so the theme→brief link does not yet vouch for that specific result.
