Baselines · Public Brief

Even with today's water management strategy unchanged, four projected hydroclimates produce different outcomes from the historical baseline in eight of nine areas of California's water system.

1. Headline

COEQWAL's water model paired today's California water management strategy with five hydroclimates: a historical baseline and four projected futures (Warmer and Drier I, Warmer and Drier II, TaiESM1 and EC-Earth3-Veg). In eight of nine areas of the water system, the modeled outcome lands in a different category. The largest changes are in freshwater for delta exports and environmental flows. One area holds steady across all five hydroclimates. Because the strategy doesn't change in this brief, what you're seeing is what the climate alone does to today's water system.

2. What this is

This is the results brief for the Understanding Today's Water System theme — the page where that theme's framing is turned into modeled outcomes. The theme explains why current operations is COEQWAL's reference point; this brief shows what the model produces when you hold today's operations fixed and vary the climate.

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model runs as scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time). To explore "what if today's rules continued under a different future?", you'd pair today's strategy with a projected hydroclimate. To explore "what if we changed how groundwater is managed?", you'd pair a different strategy with the same hydroclimate.

The model sorts outcomes into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

This brief looks at one strategy — today's operating rules, kept in place — paired with five hydroclimates: a historical baseline and four projected futures (Warmer and Drier I, Warmer and Drier II, TaiESM1 and EC-Earth3-Veg). That's five scenarios in total, all sharing the same strategy, so any difference between them comes from the hydroclimate, not from how the system is being run. Outcomes are reported for nine areas of the water system. No filter is applied.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, an outcome change between scenarios could in principle come from two places: changes in the strategy used to run the system, or changes in the hydroclimate the system is run under. COEQWAL's other briefs report scenarios where the strategy changes — and in those briefs, the model can separate an outcome change into a strategy part and a hydroclimate part.

In this brief, the strategy doesn't change. It's held constant by design across all five scenarios. So every outcome change reported here comes from the climate alone. Read alongside the other briefs, BASELINES is the climate-only reference: the part of the change the climate causes, separated out from anything operating rules can do.

5. The pattern across the system

Looking across all nine areas in Figure 1, eight areas shift to a different tier under the projected hydroclimates and one holds steady across all five. Among the shifting areas, three change in a single direction reading across the figure's columns. The remaining five shift in less predictable ways — community water systems, environmental flows, freshwater for delta exports, reservoir storage and groundwater storage do not move in a single direction across the projected hydroclimates.

Because all the variation here comes from the climate (see §4), this is a reading of which areas are most sensitive to climate under today's strategy. It is not a reading of strategy tradeoffs — the cost of one management choice against another — because the strategy doesn't change here. The system's standing tradeoffs among competing uses are still present; what this brief shows is which of them climate stress sharpens, not what any operating decision adds or removes.

6. About specific places

Eight of the nine areas summarize many specific places across California — what the model calls Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Cities, reservoirs, river reaches, farm regions, groundwater basins, and stretches of the Delta are all tracked individually inside the sector summaries. Winter-run Chinook salmon is the exception — it's tracked as a single population, not as a summary over many places.

An area's tier averages across all the LOIs in that area. That means an area can look stable at the summary level while containing individual places under stress, or look stressed while containing places that are doing well. To see how a specific place is modeled — a particular community, river stretch, or basin — the data explorer lets you look at outcomes one LOI at a time. This brief stays at the summary level.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports what the model produces under specific assumptions. It doesn't predict what will happen, judge whether the outcomes are acceptable, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for a particular community, decision, or person is not something the model itself decides.

The outcomes shown reflect today's water management strategy paired with the historical baseline and four modeled projected hydroclimates. They don't span every possible hydroclimate, don't include alternative ways of operating the system (those are in the other briefs), and don't go below the area-level summary. For specific places, see §6 and the explorer.
