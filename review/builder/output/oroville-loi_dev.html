Reservoir storage

Modeled tier outcomes at Oroville Reservoir

1. Headline

At Oroville Reservoir, modeled tier values across the 92 COEQWAL scenarios range from Tier 1 to Tier 4. Of the 69 scenarios run under warmer-drier hydroclimates, 11 (16%) keep the same tier label their strategy has under today's climate and 58 (84%) shift to a different tier. Because the reported scenarios vary both the operating strategy and the hydroclimate, differences between scenarios combine climate and operations effects. These effects are addressed in section 4.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model outputs are in the form of scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time). These results are sorted into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

The model also tracks specific places in the study domain, which COEQWAL terms Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Cities, reservoirs, river reaches, farm regions, groundwater basins, and stretches of the Delta are each tracked individually. Every LOI sits inside one of nine area-level summaries COEQWAL uses to organize results (community water, agriculture, environmental flows, the Delta, salmon, in-Delta freshwater, Delta exports, reservoir storage, and groundwater).

This brief reports modeled tier values for one LOI — Oroville Reservoir (reservoir storage area) across 92 scenarios from the COEQWAL library. It consists of 23 water management strategies paired with 4 hydroclimates, and is the same set used for every LOI brief.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a difference in tier value between two scenarios can come from two places: changes in the strategy used to run the system, or changes in the hydroclimate the system is run under. At an LOI like Oroville Reservoir, both axes are reported, so the difference between scenarios can be separated into a strategy part and a hydroclimate part.

Holding the hydroclimate fixed at today's climate, the spread among operating strategies at Oroville Reservoir is the strategy effect: 19 of the 23 strategies sit at Tier 1, while 2 sit at Tier 2, while 1 sits at Tier 3, while 1 sits at Tier 4. Holding the strategy fixed at current operations, the change across hydroclimates is the climate effect: the tier value moves from 1.94 (Tier 1) under today's climate to 2.27 (Tier 2) under Warmer and Drier I, 2.77 (Tier 2) under Warmer and Drier II, 3.81 (Tier 3) under TaiESM1 (label TBD). The remaining cells in the matrix combine both effects; Figure 2 separates them for every scenario.

6. About this place

Oroville Reservoir sits on the Feather River in the Sierra Nevada foothills near the city of Oroville, north of Sacramento, behind Oroville Dam — the tallest dam in the United States. It is operated by the California Department of Water Resources as the principal storage reservoir of the State Water Project (SWP), with a total capacity of about 3,425 TAF. Its primary functions are water supply, flood control, hydropower, and flows for the lower Feather River.

Location Feather River, near Oroville, north of Sacramento
System State Water Project (SWP)
Type Reservoir
Sector Reservoir Storage (RES)
Capacity ≈ 3,425 TAF (total storage)
Variable End-of-month storage (TAF)

As the SWP's principal reservoir, Oroville captures Feather River runoff, and its end-of-month storage is the variable the model tracks here. Releases pass down the Feather River to the Sacramento River and the Delta, where SWP facilities deliver water to service areas across the state — so storage at this reservoir shapes both statewide SWP supply and conditions in the lower Feather River.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports the tier results COEQWAL has generated based on what the CalSim 3 model produces under specific assumptions for one place. It doesn't predict what will happen at Oroville Reservoir, provide judgments about the modeled tier values, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for any particular community, decision, or person is not something the model itself decides.

The tier values shown reflect 92 scenarios from the COEQWAL library — pairings of 23 strategies with 4 hydroclimates — at this single LOI. They don't span every possible scenario, don't quantify uncertainty around the modeled values, and don't characterize other places. Other LOIs in the reservoir storage sector, in different parts of the system, may show different patterns; see the area summary in the theme briefs and the explorer for the full LOI set. This dev build additionally uses data that predates the project's data lock: values may still change before publication, and the names shown for the two newest hydroclimates are placeholders pending team confirmation.
