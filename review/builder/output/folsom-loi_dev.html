Reservoir storage

Modeled tier outcomes at Folsom Reservoir

1. Headline

At Folsom Reservoir, modeled tier values across the 115 COEQWAL scenarios range from Tier 1 to Tier 4. Of the 92 scenarios run under warmer-drier hydroclimates, 79 (86%) keep the same tier label their strategy has under today's climate and 13 (14%) shift to a different tier. Because the reported scenarios vary both the operating strategy and the hydroclimate, differences between scenarios combine climate and operations effects. These effects are addressed in section 4.

2. What this is

COEQWAL uses CalSim 3 — the state's main water-system model — to test how California's water system performs under different conditions. The model outputs are in the form of scenarios: each scenario is a pairing of a water management strategy (a way of operating the system, like today's rules or a proposed alternative) and a hydroclimate (patterns of temperature, rainfall, and snow that determine how much water is available over time). These results are sorted into four levels, called tiers: Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, and Critical. Tiers describe what the model produces; they don't tell anyone what to do about it.

The model also tracks specific places in the study domain, which COEQWAL terms Locations of Interest, or LOIs. Cities, reservoirs, river reaches, farm regions, groundwater basins, and stretches of the Delta are each tracked individually. Every LOI sits inside one of nine area-level summaries COEQWAL uses to organize results (community water, agriculture, environmental flows, the Delta, salmon, in-Delta freshwater, Delta exports, reservoir storage, and groundwater).

This brief reports modeled tier values for one LOI — Folsom Reservoir (reservoir storage area) across 115 scenarios from the COEQWAL library. It consists of 23 water management strategies paired with 5 hydroclimates, and is the same set used for every LOI brief.

4. Operations and climate

Because every scenario pairs a strategy with a hydroclimate, a difference in tier value between two scenarios can come from two places: changes in the strategy used to run the system, or changes in the hydroclimate the system is run under. At an LOI like Folsom Reservoir, both axes are reported, so the difference between scenarios can be separated into a strategy part and a hydroclimate part.

Holding the hydroclimate fixed at today's climate, the spread among operating strategies at Folsom Reservoir is the strategy effect: 21 of the 23 strategies sit at Tier 1, while 2 sit at Tier 4. Holding the strategy fixed at current operations, the change across hydroclimates is the climate effect: the tier value moves from 1.63 (Tier 1) under today's climate to 1.76 (Tier 1) under Warmer and Drier I, 1.94 (Tier 1) under Warmer and Drier II, 2.00 (Tier 1) under TaiESM1, 1.74 (Tier 1) under EC-Earth3-Veg — movement the tier label alone does not show, since the label holds at Tier 1 throughout. The remaining cells in the matrix combine both effects; Figure 2 separates them for every scenario.

Figure 2 shows this decomposition for every scenario. Each dot is one modeled strategy under one hydroclimate; current operations (s0020) is gold. Within each column, strategies keep a fixed left-to-right order, so a strategy occupies the same relative position in every column and can be followed across the 5 hydroclimates.

Read against the BASELINES theme brief, the climate column at Folsom Reservoir under current operations is the climate-only reference for this place. 5. Pattern across scenarios

Looking across the full scenario matrix in Figure 1, modeled tier labels at Folsom Reservoir stay between Tier 1 and Tier 4. Within each hydroclimate column: under Today's climate, strategies span Tier 1 to Tier 4; under Warmer and Drier I, strategies span Tier 1 to Tier 4; under Warmer and Drier II, strategies span Tier 1 to Tier 4; under TaiESM1, strategies span Tier 1 to Tier 4; under EC-Earth3-Veg, strategies span Tier 1 to Tier 3.

6. About this place

Folsom Reservoir sits on the American River in the Sierra Nevada foothills northeast of Sacramento, and is operated by the Bureau of Reclamation as part of the federal Central Valley Project (CVP). Its primary functions are water supply, flood control, hydropower, and instream flows. The reservoir has a total storage capacity of about 975 TAF; releases pass downstream through the lower American River toward the Sacramento area and the Delta.

Type Reservoir
Sector Reservoir Storage (RES)
Capacity ≈ 975 TAF (total storage)
Variable End-of-month storage (TAF)

As a CVP reservoir, Folsom captures American River runoff for water supply, hydropower, and flood-control space, and its end-of-month storage is the variable the model tracks here. The stored volume reflects the balance between inflow from the upper American River watershed and releases for downstream deliveries, instream flows, and flood management — so storage at this reservoir shapes water availability for the surrounding service area and conditions in the lower American River.

8. What this doesn't say

This brief reports the tier results COEQWAL has generated based on what the CalSim 3 model produces under specific assumptions for one place. It doesn't predict what will happen at Folsom Reservoir, provide judgments about the modeled tier values, or recommend any course of action. The tier names (Optimal, Acceptable, At-risk, Critical) describe modeled conditions; what they mean for any particular community, decision, or person is not something the model itself decides.

The tier values shown reflect 115 scenarios from the COEQWAL library — pairings of 23 strategies with 5 hydroclimates — at this single LOI. They don't span every possible scenario, don't quantify uncertainty around the modeled values, and don't characterize other places. Other LOIs in the reservoir storage sector, in different parts of the system, may show different patterns; see the area summary in the theme briefs and the explorer for the full LOI set. This dev build additionally uses data that predates the project's data lock: values may still change before publication, and the names shown for the two newest hydroclimates are placeholders pending team confirmation.
