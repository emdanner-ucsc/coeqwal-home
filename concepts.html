COEQWAL core concepts

The ideas the project is built on, one short entry each. Source document last updated 2026-06-12 10:01 AM PDT. Back to working pages.

A curated list of the ideas the project is built on — for personal reference and for sharing with collaborators. Each concept gets a name, a short plain-language statement, and a pointer to the canonical doc that treats it fully.

A. What the project is

1. Equity of access to planning information. The water planning tools the state actually uses (CalSim) have been inaccessible to most communities, especially those historically excluded from decision-making. COEQWAL's founding purpose is to open that information up — not to advocate for any particular strategy.

2. Participatory scenario planning. Scenarios are not produced in isolation: strategies are developed and run, results are brought to community partners in workshops, and feedback shapes the next round of scenarios. The scenario library is the product of an iterative loop, not a one-time modeling exercise.

3. Non-directive framing. COEQWAL shows what each strategy does to each part of the system and lets people weigh the trade-offs by their own values. The project never recommends a scenario, and cross-sector comparisons are always stated two-sidedly (a gain for one use is named alongside the cost to another).

B. The modeling foundation

4. CalSim as the engine. The same model the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation and DWR use for actual planning, representing the Central Valley water system as a network: inflows, reservoir storage and releases, deliveries. COEQWAL runs it many times under different rules and climates. 100-year simulation period.

5. Scenario = strategy × hydroclimate. Every scenario pairs one water management strategy (the rules, priorities, and policies for allocating water) with one hydroclimate (the temperature, rain, and snow that determine how much water there is). The pairing is the unit of analysis; results belong to the pair, never to a strategy alone.

6. The scenario library. 24 strategies × 3 served hydroclimates (Historical, Warmer and Drier I, Warmer and Drier II), spanning current-operations variants, groundwater limits (SGMA), environmental and salmon-friendly flows, community water system priorities, Delta outflow levels, and Delta conveyance. Two further climates are run but not yet served.

7. Current operations as the anchor (s0020). One strategy — existing operational rules as specified by DWR in 2023 — serves as the reference all other strategies are measured against. In user-facing language it is always "current operations," never "baseline operations."

C. How results are measured

8. Nine sectors. The system's results are tracked for nine parts of the water system: community water systems, agricultural water, environmental flows, Bay-Delta estuary ecology, winter-run Chinook salmon, freshwater for Delta exports, freshwater for in-Delta uses, reservoir storage, and groundwater storage. Disparate physical quantities, deliberately chosen so that cities, farms, and the environment are all visible at once.

9. The universal tier scale. Every sector's results are translated onto the same four-step scale — 1 Optimal, 2 Acceptable, 3 At-risk, 4 Critical — using sector-specific thresholds. The tier scale is the project's commensurability device: it is what lets salmon abundance and agricultural revenue appear on the same chart.

10. Two result pathways: tiers and raw model output. The raw CalSim output for a scenario is monthly values over a 100-year simulation, in specialist units (acre-feet, cubic feet per second) the public cannot readily translate. Tiers are an intentional simplification of that record — they trade resolution for legibility, and they carry the interpretation throughout the main website. The raw outputs are not discarded: they are served in the website's In-depth explorer for users who need full resolution. One set of model runs, two renderings — the tier pathway answers "what does this mean?", the in-depth pathway answers "what exactly happened in the model?" and keeps every tier traceable to the record it summarizes.

11. Tiers are calibrated within sectors, not across them. Tier 2 for environmental flows does not mean the same thing as Tier 2 for community deliveries — each sector's thresholds are its own. Within a sector, tiers are comparable across locations, strategies, and climates; across sectors, only the qualitative reading ("both are in the acceptable zone") is safe. This caution limits what arithmetic is defensible.

12. Ordinal tiers, continuous positions. Tiers are categories, but a location can sit anywhere within its band; continuous tier values in [1, 4.99] record where. The integer part always agrees with the assigned tier (floor constraint); the fraction adds within-band resolution.

13. Locations and aggregates. Results exist at two levels: individual locations of interest (LOIs — specific communities, districts, reaches, basins; e.g. 140 community water systems, 42 groundwater basins) and sector summaries aggregated over them. Every aggregate can hide variation among its locations — which is why both levels are kept.

D. How comparisons work (the analytical core)

14. The baseline. s0020 × Historical climate — the one scenario that represents the actual, lived status quo. "Baseline" without a modifier always means this and only this.

15. Climate-projected baselines. Current operations run forward under each projected climate: where we'd be if the climate changed but we changed nothing about how we manage water. These are full model runs, not adjustments, and they are the matched reference points that make strategy effects measurable under future climates.

16. The climate/operations decomposition. Any scenario's difference from today separates into two parts: the climate effect (ΔHC — how much the starting point moves when climate alone changes) and the operations effect (Δop — what the strategy adds or subtracts, measured against the matched climate-projected baseline). This separation is the project's central analytical move; most visualizations are renderings of it.

17. The decomposition belongs to the tier pathway, not to raw model output. The climate/operations split is shown on tier values, where it works because tiers are coarse: the categories absorb the entanglement between the two effects, and the step order (climate first, then operations) is openly a storytelling convention. Raw CalSim output does not support the same split. The model is full of hard limits — flood rules, dead pool, contract maximums — so the operations effect measured in acre-feet depends on which climate it is measured under; the two parts do not add up to the total, and what is left over can be as large as either part. A strategy can also look ineffective simply because the system is pinned against a limit, which is the wrong conclusion to hand a reader. The In-depth explorer therefore offers paired comparisons — vary exactly one of strategy, climate, or location while the others stay fixed — and never an additive climate + operations breakdown. Users who have seen the split in the tier views should not expect it on the raw data: the omission is principled, not a missing feature, and the website needs to say so where the two pathways meet.

18. Every choice has trade-offs. Water is limited, so every strategy redistributes it; no strategy moves all nine sectors in the same direction. The pattern of gains and costs across sectors — who benefits, who bears the cost, by how much — is the primary description of a strategy.

19. Robustness across climate futures. A strategy's effect can hold up across the modeled climates, shrink, or even flip sign (helping a sector under one climate, harming it under another). Whether a benefit survives climate change is a separate question from whether it exists today, and the scenario library is structured to answer it.

20. Compounding burden. The starting position matters as much as the change: a cost imposed on a sector or community already at Tier 3–4 is qualitatively different from the same cost imposed on one performing well. Reading direction and starting position together is built into the guided narratives.

E. Equity concepts

21. Equity as spread, not level. A scenario is more equitable the closer its sectors and locations sit to the same tier. Equity is a property of the distribution — a scenario with everything at Tier 2 is more equitable than one mixing Tier 1 and Tier 4 at the same average.

22. Two distinct equity questions. Cross-sector equity (does a strategy systematically favor some parts of the system over others?) and within-sector equity (within one sector, are some communities left behind while the average improves?) are independent — a scenario can be fair across sectors and deeply unequal inside one. Neither subsumes the other.

23. Averages can hide the worst-off. A sector mean can improve while a tail of small, rural, or historically excluded communities stays at Tier 4. "Percent of locations at Critical" is the most direct public-facing equity measure, and visualizations are obligated to surface, not minimize, the worst-off locations.

F. How it's communicated

24. Plain-language discipline. A small controlled vocabulary, used verbatim: "current operations" (never "baseline" alone in public text), "alternative operations" (never "proposal"), named climates ("Warmer and Drier I", never "HC1"), named tiers (never bare numbers), "sector" rather than "outcome" in new writing. Terminology is a design surface, maintained as deliberately as the visuals.

G. Future directions

Concepts that are part of the project's thinking but not expected in the launch product. Held here rather than discarded; when one ships, move it back into the conceptual section it belongs to (these two would return to F).

25. The Landscape. The umbrella conceptual frame for seeing the whole result space at once: each sector's position plotted against its own matched baseline, so that distance from the no-change reference is the strategy's effect and movement of the reference itself is the climate's effect. The interactive tool is the expert instrument; public surfaces would derive narrower views from it.

26. Guided questions, not free exploration. Public surfaces lead with the questions people actually have ("who is already under stress?", "does the benefit survive climate change?", "who gets left behind?") and present one prepared view per question, rather than handing visitors the full control surface.
