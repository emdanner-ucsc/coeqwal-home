Water map — basemap licensing options

An open item on the map's pre-publication checklist · written 2026-07-11

Status: open — decision pending

What the basemap is

The basemap is the light-gray background of streets, city names, and terrain that the water map's rivers and reservoirs are drawn on top of. It is not stored in the project: every time a visitor pans or zooms, their browser fetches small square images ("tiles") from CARTO, a mapping company, using CARTO's free "Positron" style. The map credits "© OpenStreetMap © CARTO" in the corner, as CARTO's terms require.

The issue

Whether we are licensed to keep using CARTO's free tiles on a public site is ambiguous. CARTO's FAQ says the basemaps are free for non-commercial use "by CARTO grantees" — a program one applies to — with details deferred to their basemaps page and legal terms. A modest-traffic academic page with proper attribution is very unlikely to draw complaints, and the map caps zoom at level 12, which keeps tile usage light. This is a diligence item, not a danger — but it should be settled before the map is promoted widely.

Options

1 · Ask CARTO / apply to their grants programpreferred

A public-interest university water project is exactly who the program is for. Free, zero code changes, and explicit permission in writing. Small administrative effort.

2 · Switch to a clearly-licensed free providerfallback

OpenStreetMap's own tile server explicitly allows light use on public sites with attribution — a one-line code change, but the style is busier and more colorful, so the data layers would compete with the background. Stadia Maps offers Positron-like clean styles on a free non-commercial tier, but requires an API key and adds a new company dependency.

3 · Self-host the basemapmost permanent

Download a one-file basemap of California (Protomaps format), keep it in the repository next to everything else, and depend on no outside company at all. Fully consistent with the map's static-files philosophy, but a real chunk of work to swap the map renderer — worth it only if option 1 fails.

4 · US government tiles (USGS)public domain

Zero licensing worry, ever — but the topographic style is visually heavy for a map whose whole design goal is a calm background.

Recommendation

Send CARTO a short note or grant application describing the project; treat options 2–3 as fallbacks if they decline or don't reply. No urgency at current traffic levels, but it should be resolved before wide public promotion.
